From Hope to Hostility: A 60-Year Legal Shift in Abortion Access

A detailed legal chronology of key court cases, federal regulations and other events that shaped, eroded, and ultimately overturned one's Constitutional right to abortion care.

Visualizing the volatility of legal protections, establishment, and current hostility against bodily autonomy after Roe v. Wade was overturned.

Chronological Timeline

1965

Griswold v. Connecticut

The Court recognized a right to marital privacy for contraception use.

This laid the constitutional privacy groundwork for the later Roe decision.

1972

Eisenstadt v. Baird

Contraception rights were extended to individuals, including unmarried persons.

The ruling expanded the principle of bodily autonomy, building on Griswold.

1973

Roe v. Wade

The Court recognized a constitutional right to pre-viability abortion and established the trimester framework.

This established nationwide legalization before viability.

1973

Doe v. Bolton

The ruling established a broad health exception and struck down procedural hurdles to access.

This expanded practical access to care and reinforced Roe.

1976

Planned Parenthood v. Danforth

The Court struck down spousal consent requirements and limited blanket parental consent for minors.

This protected patient decisional autonomy.

1976

Hyde Amendment (Regulation)

This legislative provision barred the use of federal funds (Medicaid) for most abortions.

This created a major financial barrier for low-income patients.

1977

Maher v. Roe

The Court ruled that states may deny Medicaid funding for most abortions.

This reduced access for low-income patients and distinguished state funding from the right to choose.

1980

Harris v. McRae

The Court upheld the Hyde Amendment's limits on federal funding for abortions.

This confirmed a major financial barrier for low-income patients, following Maher.

1983

City of Akron v. Akron Center for Reproductive Health

The Court struck down various consent, waiting period, and hospital rules as inconsistent with Roe.

This shielded access from burdensome regulations and reaffirmed Roe.

1986

Thornburgh v. American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists

The Court invalidated Pennsylvania informed-consent and reporting mandates.

This blocked provisions designed to deter patients and reaffirmed Roe.

1989

Webster v. Reproductive Health Services

The Court allowed 20-week viability testing and barred public funds or facilities from being used for abortions.

This expanded state leeway without overruling Roe, limiting the decision's scope.

1991

Rust v. Sullivan

The Court upheld the Title X counseling "gag rule."

This restricted the information that could be provided in low-income family planning clinics.

1992

Planned Parenthood of Southeastern Pennsylvania v. Casey

The Court reaffirmed the core right but adopted the undue-burden test for state restrictions.

This preserved the core right to abortion but lowered the legal standard, making it significantly easier for states to pass restrictive laws.

1993

Bray v. Alexandria Women's Health Clinic

The Court ruled that clinic blockades based on anti-abortion animus did not qualify as a conspiracy under the relevant statute.

This weakened the availability of federal civil-rights remedies against clinic obstruction.

2000

Stenberg v. Carhart

The Court struck down a state ban on a specific abortion procedure because it lacked an exception for the health of the mother.

This protected providers and patients and applied the principles of Casey.

2003

Scheidler v. National Organization for Women

The Court ruled that protest conduct did not constitute "extortion" under the Hobbs Act.

This reduced the ability to use the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO) against clinic obstruction.

2006

Scheidler v. National Organization for Women

The Court reaffirmed that the same theory could not be used under RICO or the Hobbs Act.

This further limited the tools available to prevent clinic obstruction.

2006

Ayotte v. Planned Parenthood

The Court instructed lower courts to narrowly remedy issues to salvage a parental-notice law.

This encouraged minimalist remedies, allowing more restrictive state laws to survive challenges.

2007

Gonzales v. Carhart

The Court upheld a federal ban on a specific procedure, even without an explicit health exception.

This increased judicial deference to legislatures, allowing the federal government to ban a procedure without including a health exception.

2014

McCullen v. Coakley

The Court struck down 35-foot clinic buffer zones on First Amendment grounds.

This made it more difficult for clinics to manage protests and ensure patient access.

2014

Burwell v. Hobby Lobby Stores

The Religious Freedom Restoration Act was ruled to allow closely-held firms to refuse Affordable Care Act contraception coverage.

This reduced access to no-cost contraception for some workers by expanding religious exemptions.

2016

Whole Woman’s Health v. Hellerstedt

The Court struck down Texas laws requiring admitting privileges at hospitals and ambulatory surgical center standards for clinics.

This reopened the path to challenge burdensome regulations and clarified the undue-burden test from Casey.

2018

National Institute of Family and Life Advocates v. Becerra

The Court barred compelled notices by crisis-pregnancy centers.

This weakened the tools used to mandate disclosure by crisis-pregnancy centers.

2019

Box v. Planned Parenthood of Indiana and Kentucky

The Court upheld Indiana's law regarding the disposal of fetal remains but did not reach the core issue of a "reason-based" ban.

This validated the state's ancillary regulation on fetal remains.

2020

June Medical Services L.L.C. v. Russo

The Court struck down a Louisiana admitting-privileges law, but the majority was fractured on its methodology.

This temporarily preserved access, but the narrow legal reasoning signaled increasing judicial tolerance for restrictive state laws.

2020

Little Sisters of the Poor Saints Peter and Paul Home v. Pennsylvania

The Court upheld broad religious and moral exemptions to the Affordable Care Act's contraception mandate.

This allowed more employer opt-outs from the mandate, extending the Hobby Lobby ruling.

2021

Whole Woman’s Health v. Jackson

The Court allowed only a narrow pre-enforcement suit against Texas Senate Bill 8, and the law largely remained in effect.

The law effectively ceased in-state abortion access pre-Dobbs and tolerated aggressive state legal design.

2022

Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization

The Supreme Court ruled that the Constitution confers no abortion right and explicitly overruled Roe v. Wade and Planned Parenthood v. Casey.

This returned all regulation of abortion to the states, causing trigger bans to take immediate effect.

2023-2025+

Post-Roe: Court Cases

Major conflicts in the courts focus on medication abortion access (challenging Federal Drug Administration authority over mifepristone) and states attempting to criminalize or restrict interstate travel for care.

The battle centers on federal authority versus interstate commerce conflicts.

2023-2025+

Post-Roe: Regulations

Conservative states enact shield laws to block providers from complying with anti-abortion states' legal requests, while others attempt to impose novel restrictions on telehealth and mail-order abortion pills.

The US Postal Service is also a point of contention regarding delivery of mifepristone and misoprostol.

2023-2025+

Post-Roe: Public Health Crises

The US, which already faces the highest maternal mortality rate among wealthy nations, sees this crisis worsen as restrictions take effect. Legal constraints mean emergency care is delayed or denied for patients.

The situation exacerbates risks for vulnerable populations, including victims of sexual assault, and minors, who may be legally compelled to carry non-viable or high-risk pregnancies, creating profound physical and mental health traumas.
